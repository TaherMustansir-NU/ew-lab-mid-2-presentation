PRESENTATION SCRIPT  
Deepfake Technology: Innovation or Threat?  
Speakers:  Taher  |  Noman  
Duration: ~5 Minutes  |  Format: Two -Person with PowerPoint Slides  
Audience: University Teachers and Students  
 
FORMATTING KEY  
Bold text   =  Words to emphasize with your voice  
[Stage directions in italics]   =  Delivery notes / actions  
  
SLIDE 1: Title Slide — Deepfake Technology: Innovation or Threat?  
TAHER    (Greetings + Attention Grabber)  
[Both speakers walk to the front. Taher stands centre -left, Noman stands centre -right. Taher makes confident eye 
contact with the audience before speaking.]  
 
Good morning, everyone.  
 
[Pause — let the room settle.]  
 
Before we say anything else — I want you to watch something on the screen.  
[Click — a short deepfake video plays: a well -known figure (e.g. a news anchor or public personality) saying 
something completely false. Let it play for 8 –10 seconds.]  
 
That video is completely fake.  That person never  said those words. It was made by an AI — in under 
one hour.  
 
My name is Taher, and this is my partner Noman.  
Today, we are here to talk about one of the most powerful  — and one of the most dangerous  — 
technologies of our time:  
Deepfake Technology.  
 
 
SLIDE 2: What Is a Deepfake? — Introduction of Idea  
NOMAN    (Introduce the Idea + Knowledge about the Idea)  
[Noman steps slightly forward. Click — slide shows a side -by-side comparison: a real face on the left, an AI -
generated version on the right.]  
 
So — what exactly is a deepfake?  
 
A deepfake is a piece of media — a video, an image, or even audio — where a person's face or voice 
has been replaced by artificial intelligence  to look and sound completely real.  
The name itself comes from two words: "deep learning"  and "fake."  
 
[Click — slide shows a simple diagram: Images fed into AI → AI learns → Fake video output.]  
 
The technology works by feeding an AI thousands of images of a real person. The AI studies every 
angle, every expression, every detail  — and then generates brand new content that mimics them — 
perfectly.  
 
And here is the most alarming part:  
You cannot  tell the difference with your naked eye.  
 
Now — this technology does have genuine, positive uses.  It is used in film production, in virtual 
education, and even in medical training.  
But like every powerful tool — it has a very dark side.  
 
 
SLIDE 3: The Human Cost — Storytelling + Problem  
TAHER    (Storytelling + Problem Discussion)  
[Taher steps forward. Tone shifts — slower, more serious. Click — slide shows a blurred news headline: 
'Deepfake Victim Speaks Out.']  
 
Let me tell you a story.  
 
In 2023, a young woman in Pakistan woke up one morning to find videos of herself — videos she had 
never made  — being shared across social media. She had done nothing wrong. She was just a normal 
person going about her life.  
But within days — her reputation , her career , and her mental health  were destroyed. She was a 
deepfake victim.  
 
[Pause. Let the gravity sink in. Then click — slide shows statistics.]  
 
This is not a rare case. There were over 500,000 deepfake videos  circulating online in 2023 — a rise 
of 550% in just four years.  
 
Deepfakes are being used to:  
Spread false news  and political lies.  
Manipulate elections.  
Blackmail and destroy ordinary people.  
And create content that no one ever consented to.  
 
The problem is not just technical. It is deeply, painfully human.  
 
 
SLIDE 4: Fighting Back — The Solutions  
NOMAN    (Solution Proposed)  
[Noman steps forward with a more hopeful, energetic tone. Click — slide shows three pillars labeled: 1. AI 
Detection  2. Digital Watermarking  3. Media Literacy.]  
 
But here is the good news — 
We are not  helpless.  
 
There are three key solutions being developed and used right now.  
 
First — AI Detection Tools.  Companies like Microsoft and Intel have built systems that scan videos 
and flag deepfakes with up to 96% accuracy.  AI fighting AI — on our side.  
 
Second — Digital Watermarking.  Every piece of AI -generated content can now be tagged at the 
source — like a hidden signature — so that platforms can trace and remove  fake content before it 
spreads.  
 
And third — the most powerful solution of all,  and the one most relevant to the people in this room:  
Media Literacy.  Teaching people — students, teachers, citizens — to question what they see.  To 
verify sources. To pause before sharing anything that shocks or angers them.  
 
Because an informed person is a protected  person.  
 
 
SLIDE 5: Why This Matters for Us — Unique Value Proposition  
TAHER    (Unique Value Proposition)  
[Taher steps forward. Tone becomes personal and direct — speak to the room, not just to the air. Click — slide 
shows: 'Gen Z consumes 7+ hours of digital content per day.']  
 
Now let us talk about why this matters specifically to us — to this university, to this generation.  
 
We are the first generation  to grow up completely inside the digital world. We live online. We trust 
what we see. And deepfakes are designed  to exploit exactly that trust.  
 
[Click — slide shows split screen: 'Misinformed Citizens' vs. 'Digitally Literate Citizens.']  
 
Here is our unique value proposition:  
Knowledge is your shield.  
 
A person who understands deepfake technology cannot be fooled by it.  A society that understands 
this technology can demand better laws, better platforms, and better accountability  from the people in 
power.  
 
We are not just consumers of content. We are the future creators, leaders, and regulators  of it. 
And that means the way we respond to this technology today  will shape what happens tomorrow.  
 
 
SLIDE 6: What You Can Do — Call to Action  
NOMAN    (Call to Action)  
[Noman steps forward with a clear, steady voice. Maintain strong eye contact with different sections of the 
audience. Click — slide shows three numbered action points.]  
 
So — what do we want you to walk away with today? Three simple actions.  
 
Number One — Verify before you share.  If a video shocks you, angers you, or seems too dramatic to 
be true — pause.  Check the source. Look for signs of manipulation before you pass it on.  
 
Number Two — Talk about it.  Bring this conversation home. Bring it to your friends, your family, your 
study groups. Awareness is the first line of defense,  and it costs nothing.  
 
Number Three — Demand regulation.  Countries like Pakistan are still building their legal frameworks 
around AI -generated content. Your voice matters.  Push for laws that protect people from digital abuse 
— because right now, the law is behind the technology.  
 
Deepfake technology will keep growing.  
The only question is — will we grow with it?  
 
 
SLIDE 7: Closing — Conclusion (The Circle)  
TAHER    (Conclusion — Circles Back to Opening)  
[Taher walks forward. Voice becomes calm and measured. The room should feel like it is being brought full circle. 
Click — the same deepfake video from the opening plays again, briefly.]  
 
Do you remember what you saw at the very start of this presentation?  
 
[Pause — let the audience recall.]  
 
That video was fake.  Not a single second of it was real.  
But the impact  it can have on a real human being's life? That is very real.  
 
Deepfake technology is not purely  an innovation. It is not purely  a threat.  
It is a mirror — and it reflects exactly what we choose to do with it.  
 
We can use it to teach, to create, to heal.  
Or we can let it divide, deceive, and destroy.  
 
That choice — as always — belongs to us.  
 
[Taher steps back. Noman steps forward to stand beside Taher. Both face the audience.]  
 
NOMAN    (Closing Remarks)  
Thank you so much for your time and attention.  
We are happy to take any questions you may have.  
 
 
 
SPEAKER NOTES & RUBRIC ALIGNMENT  
Greetings (Slide 1)  — Taher opens with a warm "Good morning everyone" before launching into the hook.  
Attention Grabber (Slide 1)  — The deepfake video played at the very start is the hook. It creates instant shock 
and curiosity.  
Introduce the Idea + Knowledge (Slide 2)  — Noman defines deepfakes clearly with technical detail and 
examples of legitimate use.  
Problem + Storytelling (Slide 3)  — Taher tells a real -world personal story to ground the problem in human 
impact, then supports with statistics.  
Solution (Slide 4)  — Noman presents three concrete, practical solutions. Tone is hopeful and action -oriented.  
Unique Value Proposition (Slide 5)  — Taher connects the topic directly to the audience (Gen Z, university 
context) and frames 'knowledge as a shield' as the core UVP.  
Call to Action (Slide 6)  — Noman gives three clear, memorable, and doable actions. Ends on a rhetorical 
question to maintain energy.  
Conclusion — The Circle (Slide 7)  — The opening deepfake video is replayed. Taher brings the speech back to 
the exact starting point, creating a satisfying narrative circle before the powerful final line.
